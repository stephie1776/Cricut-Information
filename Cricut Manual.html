The foundation

Getting Started

Before the first cut, get to know the four things you’ll touch every single project: the machine, the mat, the blade, and the material.

Cricut machine overview

Every Cricut works the same way: a sticky mat carries your material under a blade that follows the paths from Design Space. Learn these five spots and the machine stops being mysterious.

Which machine am I on?
Explore machines cut vinyl, iron-on, paper, and cardstock — the everyday crafter set. Maker machines do all that plus fabric, leather, balsa, and chipboard using extra blades (rotary, knife). Joy / Joy Xtra are compact and great for labels and smaller decals.

Mats, blades, and tools

Mats — matched by grip

The grip must match the material’s weight. Too strong and you tear delicate paper lifting it off; too light and the material slides mid-cut.

Blades — matched by thickness

The Fine-Point blade handles ~90% of your work: vinyl, iron-on, cardstock, sticker paper. Reach for others only when the material gets thick or fabric-y.

The hand tools worth owning

  • Weeding tool — the hook that pulls away the excess vinyl you don’t want.
  • Scraper / brayer — presses material flat onto the mat and smooths vinyl onto surfaces.
  • Spatula — lifts delicate cuts off the mat without curling them.
  • Tweezers — for tiny pieces and lifting sticker corners.
  • Transfer tape — moves a weeded vinyl design onto its final surface in one piece.
Stephanie’s Tip

Buy a spare Fine-Point blade before you think you need one. A dull blade is the #1 cause of “why won’t it cut through” — and you never realize it’s dull until you’re mid-project at 9pm. Swap it, and the problem usually vanishes.

Design with cutting in mind

Procreate for Cricut

Cricut cuts along color edges, not your beautiful details. The cleaner and more deliberate your Procreate file, the cleaner the cut. Six habits make all the difference.

1 · Choosing canvas size

Set your canvas to the real-world size you want to cut. A 4-inch decal? Make a 4″×4″ canvas. This way nothing has to be guessed or rescaled later, and your DPI stays honest.

  • Standard mat = 12″ × 12″ — a safe max canvas for most cuts.
  • Print Then Cut has a smaller printable area (about 9.25″ × 6.75″, larger on newer machines — check yours).

2 · Resolution (DPI)

300 DPI gives the blade crisp edges to follow. Lower DPI is where fuzzy, stair-stepped cut lines come from.

3 · Transparent backgrounds

This is the one that trips up everyone. Before exporting, tap the Background layer off (uncheck it) so it becomes a checkerboard. That checkerboard is “nothing here” — it tells Cricut what is shape and what is empty.

4 · Layers = colors

Put each color on its own Procreate layer, exactly as you’d want it separated in Design Space. Think of one layer as one mat / one vinyl color.

5 · Exporting PNGs

Tap Actions (wrench) → Share. Two routes:

  • Share → PNG — one flattened image (background off = transparent). Good for single-color cuts and Print Then Cut.
  • Share → Share Layers → PNG files — exports each layer as its own PNG. Best for multi-color projects.

6 · Avoiding jagged edges

Stephanie’s Tip

For cut files, use the Monoline brush or a hard-edged shape — never a watercolor or textured brush. And turn off Procreate’s drawing aids that add softness. If you can see fuzzy pixels when you zoom way in, so can the blade.

Before you export from Procreate
  • Canvas is at the real cut size, 300 DPI
  • Each color is on its own layer
  • Edges are crisp — no soft or textured brushes
  • Background layer is OFF (checkerboard showing)
  • Exported as PNG (or Share Layers for multi-color)
  • File saved to the “Cricut Exports” folder in Files

iPad edition

Design Space

You design in Procreate, so you only need Design Space for a few jobs: bring the art in, set the real size, tell the machine how to cut, and use a handful of tools. Here’s where to tap.

Uploading artwork

Cut vs. Print Then Cut

This single choice changes everything downstream. Pick it when you save the upload.

Resize correctly

Tap the design, then type exact dimensions in the size fields (don’t eyeball the corner drag). Keep the lock icon closed to hold proportions. If you designed at real size in Procreate, it often imports already correct.

The core tools

Duplicate & Align

Duplicate makes copies; Align evens up spacing and edges. Use Align → Center to stack layered pieces perfectly.

Attach

Holds pieces in their exact position so the machine doesn’t rearrange them to “save material.” Essential for multi-piece layouts.

Stephanie’s Tip

Since you design in Procreate, you’ll mostly live in just three buttons: Upload, Attach, and Offset. Weld and Slice matter more for people building art inside Design Space — don’t feel behind for skipping them.

Before tapping “Make It”
  • Design is the correct real-world size
  • Right mode chosen: Cut or Print Then Cut
  • Multi-piece layouts are Attached
  • Colors are on the layers you expect
  • HTV projects: ready to Mirror on the next screen (see §5)

Adhesive projects

Vinyl Projects

Vinyl sticks to hard surfaces — mugs, laptops, walls, signs. The whole game is: cut, weed away the excess, then move what’s left onto your surface with transfer tape.

Permanent vinyl

Glossy finish. Holds up to water, sun, and washing. Reach for it on tumblers, water bottles, outdoor signs, and car decals. Harder to remove cleanly later.

Removable vinyl

Matte finish. Peels off cleanly for a year or two. Perfect for wall quotes, window décor, and anything temporary or rented.

Quick test
Glossy and you want it to last → permanent. Matte and you might remove it → removable. When unsure for décor, removable saves your walls.

Transfer tape, step by step

Layering vinyl

For multi-color designs, apply colors in stages and line them up. Use a hinge method: tape the top edge down, flip up, peel the backing, and roll it down so layers register cleanly. Cut each color separately (one color per mat).

Stephanie’s Tip

Weeding goes faster if you cut a tiny registration square in a corner of each layer (same spot on every color). Line the squares up when layering and your colors land perfectly — then weed the squares away at the end.

Vinyl project checklist
  • Right vinyl chosen — permanent (lasting) or removable (temporary)
  • Material set correctly on the machine (do NOT mirror for vinyl)
  • Vinyl placed liner-side-down on the green mat
  • Test cut done — blade just scores the vinyl, not the backing
  • Weeded, taped, and surface is clean & dry before applying

Heat-applied

Heat Transfer Vinyl

HTV (iron-on) bonds to fabric with heat. It has one rule beginners forget — and forgetting it ruins the shirt. So we’ll start there.

Mirroring — the one rule

On the Make It screen, flip the Mirror toggle on for every HTV mat. If your letters come out backwards on the shirt, this is the step that was missed.

Heat settings

Heat, time, and pressure depend on your HTV brand and the fabric. These are typical starting points — always confirm with the official Cricut Heat Guide for your exact combo.

MaterialTemp (starting)TimePeel
Cricut Everyday Iron-On~315°F / 155°C~30 secWarm
Siser EasyWeed~305°F / 150°C~10–15 secHot/Warm
Glitter / Holographic~320°F / 160°C~15–20 secCold
Pressure & prep
Preheat the blank for a few seconds to flatten and dry it. Apply firm pressure. A heat press or EasyPress beats an iron for even, lasting bonds.

Layering HTV

Press each color one at a time, lightest pressure-passes first. Between layers, do a short “tack” press (2–3 sec) rather than a full press, then do one final full press at the end. Avoid stacking more than a few layers — it gets stiff.

Care instructions

  • Wait 24 hours before the first wash.
  • Turn the garment inside out; wash cold, mild detergent.
  • Tumble dry low or hang dry. No bleach.
  • Don’t iron directly on the design — use a cloth or press inside-out.
Stephanie’s Tip

Tape a sticky note that says “MIRROR?” to your heat press. Ninety percent of ruined HTV projects are a forgotten mirror toggle — one glance saves a shirt and a sigh.

HTV project checklist
  • Mirror is ON for every HTV mat
  • HTV placed shiny side down on the green mat
  • Heat guide checked for this material + fabric
  • Blank preheated, design weeded
  • Carrier peeled at the right temp (warm/cold per type)

Full-color magic

Print Then Cut Stickers

Print Then Cut is where your Procreate art shines in full color: your home printer prints the design, then the Cricut reads registration marks and cuts neatly around it. Stickers, planner art, labels — all live here.

Sticker paper & waterproofing

  • Matte / glossy sticker paper — the everyday choice; runs through an inkjet printer.
  • Printable vinyl — more durable, slightly water-resistant.
  • Waterproofing: inkjet ink is not waterproof on its own. Laminate the sheet or seal with a clear spray/cold laminate before cutting for water-safe stickers.

Bleed — kills the white edge

Offset — the sticker border

Want that classic white (or colored) outline around a sticker? Add an Offset in Design Space. It builds a slightly larger copy of your shape behind the art, which also becomes the new cut line.

Calibration

If your cuts land slightly off the printed edge, run Print Then Cut Calibration (in Design Space settings). You print a test sheet, the machine reads it, and it dials in the blade-to-print accuracy. Do this once on a new machine and any time cuts drift.

Stephanie’s Tip

Print Then Cut struggles in dim light and on glossy paper because the sensor can’t read the marks. Cut near a bright, even light and use matte sticker paper if scans keep failing.

Sticker checklist
  • Saved as Print Then Cut on upload
  • Bleed ON in the print dialog
  • Offset added if you want a border
  • Calibration done (or re-done if cuts drift)
  • Sticker paper set as the material; laminated if waterproof needed

Cardstock & paper

Paper Crafts

Paper is forgiving, cheap to practice on, and where score lines and intricate cuts come in. Great for cards, tags, and dimensional flowers.

Greeting cards

The trick to a card that folds crisply is a score line, not a cut, down the fold. In Design Space, add a Score line and Attach it to the card shape so the machine scores and cuts in one pass.

Gift tags

Tiny and quick — perfect for using up cardstock scraps. Add a small circle, then Slice it out near the top for the string hole. Batch a whole mat full at once.

Paper flowers

Spiral-cut petals roll into roses; layered teardrop petals stack into blooms. Use green or purple mat depending on cardstock weight, and a fresh blade for clean spirals.

Stephanie’s Tip

For intricate paper cuts, turn the material setting to “Cardstock — Intricate Cuts” and use a brayer to press the paper fully onto the mat first. Lifting paper mid-cut is what tears delicate detail.

Paper project checklist
  • Score lines added and Attached to the cut shape
  • Mat grip matched to paper weight
  • Paper pressed flat with a brayer / scraper
  • Fresh-ish blade for intricate cuts
  • Use the spatula to lift finished pieces off the mat

Make it tidy

Labels & Organization

Labels are the fastest way to feel like a Cricut wizard — small, useful, and quick to batch. Design a set in Procreate, then cut a whole sheet at once.

Pantry labels

Use permanent vinyl if jars get washed, or removable if you reorganize often. Matte black or white vinyl reads cleanest. Design all labels on one Procreate canvas, each word on its own layer or grouped, then Attach so spacing holds.

Storage bins

Bigger surfaces, bigger vinyl. Removable vinyl is friendlier here since bin contents change. For fabric bins, use HTV instead of adhesive vinyl.

Custom decals

Laptops, water bottles, mailboxes, light switches — anywhere a small permanent decal adds personality. This is your sweet spot for testing new Procreate designs at low material cost.

Stephanie’s Tip

Keep a reusable Procreate “label template” file at your standard jar size. New pantry? Duplicate it, swap the words, export. You’ll cut a fresh set in five minutes flat.

Label batch checklist
  • All labels sized to the real surface
  • Whole set Attached to keep the layout
  • Permanent vs removable vinyl chosen for the use
  • Surface cleaned (alcohol wipe) before applying

When it goes wrong

Troubleshooting

Almost every problem has a boringly simple cause. Find the symptom, try the fix in order, and you’ll solve most issues without searching forums at midnight.

Quick-fix table
SymptomLikely cause & fix
White background appearsYou uploaded a JPEG or didn’t remove the background. Re-export a transparent PNG (background OFF), or use Design Space’s erase/remove-background on upload.
Fuzzy / jagged edgesLow DPI or a textured brush. Redesign at 300 DPI with solid color and a hard-edge brush. For scaling freedom, vectorize to SVG.
Cut at the wrong sizeResized by eye. Type exact dimensions in Design Space; design at real size in Procreate so it imports correct.
Cutting too deep (through backing)Pressure too high or wrong material. Pick the exact material setting; nudge pressure to “Less.”
Not cutting throughDull blade, low pressure, or wrong setting. Swap/clean the blade, set pressure to “More,” run a test cut.
Material slips / tears on matMat too sticky or not sticky enough. Refresh a worn mat (wash with water); de-tack a too-grippy mat by patting a clean cotton shirt. Use a brayer to seat material.
Bluetooth won’t connectForget the device in iPad Settings and re-pair. Stay within ~10–15 ft, close and reopen Design Space, restart the machine.
Stephanie’s Tip

When a cut fails, change one thing at a time — blade, then pressure, then mat — and test between each. Changing three at once means you never learn which one was the culprit.

Build the skill

Practice Projects

Five projects in order of difficulty. Each one adds exactly one new skill, so by the last you’ll have touched every part of the workflow.

01Simple decal

New skill: the full upload-to-apply loop. Draw a one-color shape (a heart, a word) in Procreate, cut in removable vinyl, apply to a notebook. No layering, no mirror.

02Layered decal

New skill: registration & layering. Two- or three-color design, each color its own layer/mat. Use the registration-square trick to line them up on a tumbler.

03T-shirt

New skill: mirroring & heat. Single-color HTV design — remember Mirror ON, shiny side down. Press per the heat guide, follow the 24-hour wash rule.

04Sticker sheet

New skill: Print Then Cut. A few full-color Procreate doodles, bleed on, add offsets for white borders, calibrate, print, cut. Laminate for water resistance.

05Greeting card

New skill: scoring & paper. Cardstock card with an attached score line, a sliced detail, maybe a small layered vinyl or paper accent. Combines everything.

Skill tracker
  • Project 1 — Simple decal  ·  upload & apply
  • Project 2 — Layered decal  ·  registration
  • Project 3 — T-shirt  ·  mirror & heat
  • Project 4 — Sticker sheet  ·  Print Then Cut
  • Project 5 — Greeting card  ·  scoring
Stephanie’s Tip

Keep your first version of each project, flaws and all. Coming back in a month and seeing how far your weeding, layering, and pressing have come is the most motivating thing in this whole craft.

Bonus chapter

Procreate → SVG

PNG works for most cuts. But when you want a design to scale to any size without fuzzing, keep colors perfectly separated, or sell your files, you want a vector SVG. Here’s the honest path from Procreate to a clean cut file.

Raster vs. vector — why it matters

The catch
Procreate is a raster app — it can’t export a true SVG by itself. You always pass through a converter. The good news: a free one does the job well.

The conversion flow

Your software options

ToolCostNotes
InkscapeFreeThe classic crafter route. Desktop only. Reliable Trace Bitmap tool.
Affinity DesignerFree (as of 2026)Now free, with a basic trace and SVG/DXF export — a strong no-subscription pick.
Silhouette StudioDesigner ed. paidGreat trace tool; note the free Basic edition can’t export SVG.
Adobe IllustratorSubscriptionThe pro standard. Image Trace → Expand → export SVG.

Free path, step by step (Inkscape)

  1. In Procreate, export your design as a PNG with the background off (Share → Share Layers → PNG for multi-color).
  2. Open Inkscape on your computer and drag the PNG in; click OK on the import pop-up.
  3. Select the image, then go to Path → Trace Bitmap. Adjust the threshold slider until the preview matches your shape, then apply.
  4. Delete the original raster image left underneath the new traced vector.
  5. (Optional but worth it) Select the path and choose Path → Simplify to reduce node clutter — fewer nodes means smoother cuts and easier weeding.
  6. File → Save As → SVG. Move it to your iPad (iCloud / Files), then Upload in Design Space.

When you actually need SVG (and when you don’t)

Reach for SVG

  • Multi-color layered designs
  • Anything you’ll resize a lot
  • Files you plan to sell or reuse

PNG is plenty

  • A single simple silhouette
  • Any Print Then Cut sticker
  • Quick one-off projects
Stephanie’s Tip

The cleaner your Procreate lines, the cleaner the trace. Every bump becomes an extra node. Draw with a monoline brush, keep edges crisp, and run Simplify after tracing — your future weeding self will thank you.

Vectorizing checklist
  • PNG exported with background OFF
  • Traced to a vector path (Trace Bitmap / Image Trace)
  • Original raster deleted underneath
  • Path simplified to cut node count
  • Saved as .SVG and uploaded to Design Space
